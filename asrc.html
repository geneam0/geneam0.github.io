IoT Environmental Sensors

  • Gene Lam
  • Wednesday, June 10, 2020 2:09pm

I met Dr. Ricardo Toledo-Crow at a Macaulay STEM Society event where we toured the Advanced Science Research Center. As most of the research done in the building was science-focused, I wasn't sure if I could gain engineering skills in the labs. After all, I had just started my Computer Engineering major at the time and wanted to explore careers in the field. Nevertheless, I continued learning about the interesting research done at the ASRC building and met Ricardo. Ricardo is the Director of the Next-Generation Environmental Sensors Lab. Talking to Ricardo, I was inspired by the up-and-coming projects he was working on. The lab was very new and I wanted to make a significant impact in his research.

My first days at the lab were difficult as I didn't even know how to solder. Ricardo was very patient with me, giving me copper boards to practice on as well as programming projects I could handle. One time, I was soldering parts onto a neuroscience shield and accidentally soldered on an integrated circuit without a holder. At the time, I thought the holder was necessary, and spent a grueling day trying to desolder the part with the help of my friend Natalie. In the process, we broke the integrated circuit and the shield didn't work. Ricardo was extremely forgiving, ordering another shield for me to work on. Here is a picture of the broken shield next to the working model.

My defining project was building a mesh network of cost-effective environmental sensors to deploy in Jamaica Bay, NIST in Maryland, and the Amazon Rainforest using LORA technology. I improved upon the MVP by transferring the sensors onto the Adafruit feather, a sleaker, low-cost version of the Arduino Uno. I also investigated different Internet of Things options and implemented the sensors with The Things Network Gateway as a way to gather data from the different sensors. Lastly, I bridged the gateway to The Things Board, an open-source data management platform for processing and visualizing the data. Although my focus in engineering has shifted to robotics, I owe it to Ricardo for jump-starting my engineer career. I learned to work with Arduinos, write technical documents, independently work on projects, and ask for help. Here's a picture of the CO2 level data. The red is from the sensors when they were uncallibrated and blue is actual.
