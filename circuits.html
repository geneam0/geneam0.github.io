DC Motor

Engineering 101 was one of the first classes I took that solidified my intent in pursuing computer engineering.

Here's my "inspirational" and maybe unnecessary backstory:

I entered college as a computer science major, but I never saw myself as a web developer or app developer. I liked programming but those weren't interesting to me. I knew that I enjoyed tinkering, but engineer just wasn't something I ever considered. I didn't even know what engineers did. After exploring around for a bit, I found computer engineering and used my second semester to test the waters out.

Anyways, my professor was great (albeit old-fashioned). He gave us a ton of electrical projects to inspire our impressionable minds: radios, speakers, a record player, microphones. I had so much fun.

Our final project was a simple DC motor that wasn't so simple. The theory behind it is pretty simple. When current flows through a winding wire, it induces a magnetic field powerful enough to generate torque. A motor uses this principle to rotate and spin. Heres a video that explains the physics behind it.

Physics is ideal, almost too optimistic at times. Actually applying the theory and building the project... not so much. Our first mistake happened with the wiring of the motor. The direction you wind the wires affects the direction that current flows and affects the direction of the magnetic field. Here's a video of me testing the polarity of the field magnets and armature magnets:

After we got the write polarity on the magnets, our motor still would not spin. We worked on the motor for several weeks, rebuilding the motor at least three times. Sometimes, there wasn't enough wiring. Other times, there was too much glue for current to pass. We even spoke to the lab technician and he theorized it was the brushes. We were using copper brushes so he suggested we tried aluminum, aka the paper clip. It was really a conglomerate of all these factors that prevented our motor from spinning.

The day the project was due, our motor still would not work despite all our attempts. Thankfully, our professor - clairvoyant and omniscient - helped us fix our motor. He skinned off the excessive glue layer that meddled with the commutator plates. He also exposed more wiring and thus, more current to the battery. He finally got our motor to work by ramping up the voltage of the car battery to 20V.

Success! Here is a link to the report I wrote up with nice drawings.
